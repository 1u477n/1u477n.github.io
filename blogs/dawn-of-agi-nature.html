The Dawn of AGI: Landmark Nature Study Declares Human-Level Intelligence is Already Here

In a groundbreaking perspective published this week in Nature, a multidisciplinary team of scientists and philosophers has issued a bold declaration: Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) is no longer a distant dream—it is our current reality.

The article, titled “Does AI already have human-level intelligence? The evidence is clear,” marks a historic shift in the scientific discourse surrounding machine cognition. Led by Eddy Keming Chen, Mikhail Belkin, Leon Bergen, and David Danks (UC San Diego), the authors argue that the global debate has been stalled by “dogmatic skepticism” and a misunderstanding of what intelligence truly requires.

1) A Third Cognitive Revolution

The researchers frame this moment as the “third cognitive revolution,” comparing it to the paradigm shifts of Copernicus and Darwin. Just as humanity had to accept that Earth is not the center of the universe and that humans evolved from other species, we must now recognize that “human-level” intelligence can exist within a non-biological, “alien” architecture.

Core reframing

Human-level ≠ perfect. The paper argues the conversation has been distorted by treating “general intelligence” as “superintelligence.” Humans aren’t perfect — general intelligence is about flexible competence across diverse domains.

2) What “Human-Level” Means in Their Definition

“We often conflate general intelligence with superintelligence or perfection,” explains lead author Eddy Keming Chen. “But individual humans are not perfect; they don't know everything. What defines general intelligence is flexible, broad competence across diverse domains—and current Large Language Models (LLMs) have clearly demonstrated this.”

2.1 Broad competence across domains

The perspective emphasizes generalization: the ability to adapt, reason, and synthesize across unfamiliar problems and many disciplines, rather than optimizing for a single narrow benchmark.

3) The Evidence is Irrefutable (According to the Authors)

The authors point to a 2025 milestone where advanced models, such as GPT-4.5, officially passed the Turing Test — with human judges mistaking the AI for a person 73% of the time, outperforming actual human participants.

3.1 Beyond imitation: reasoning and synthesis

Beyond mere imitation, the article highlights the models' ability to perform complex biological reasoning, solve novel mathematical problems, and synthesize information across thousands of disciplines simultaneously.

4) From Fear to Partnership

Rather than viewing AGI as a threat to “human exceptionalism,” the Nature piece calls for an inspiring new era of collaboration. By recognizing these systems as generally intelligent, the scientific community can move from asking if they are smart to asking how we can best partner with them to solve the world’s most pressing challenges.

A pragmatic next step

Shift the question: from “Is it intelligent?” to “How do we safely collaborate?” — with governance, evaluation, and human-centered deployment.

5) The Closing Note

“The evidence is no longer in question,” the authors conclude. “We have built machines that can think with us. The real innovation now lies in how we choose to move forward with this new form of intelligence—with compassionate curiosity rather than anxious evasion.”
